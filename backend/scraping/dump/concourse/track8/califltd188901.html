This is an example of railroading in the late 1880s. You may think the number of station stops a bit excessive, but it is right in line with other trains of the period; the 1889 precursor to the Sunset Limited made 253 stops on its daily journey from New Orleans to San Francisco—104 in Texas alone, including dozens in the most barren stretches of West Texas. To be fair, it must be noted that many of those "stations"—especially those which do not show to have either a ticket office or a telegraph operator—were probably nothing more than a sign by the tracks denoting a place for trains to stop, while the large number of scheduled stops worked as an aid to the regional dispatchers who had the responsibility to send out non-timetable local freights which had to work their territories while remaining clear of the higher-priority express trains in an era before radio.

At the time of this timetable, the Santa Fe system had only recently been pieced together into a close approximation of its modern form as a system connecting Chicago with the Gulf and Pacific coasts under a single management.  Through service from Kansas City to Los Angeles had begun on May 31, 1887; service south to Galveston via Texas affiliate Gulf, Colorado & Santa Fe began on June 18 of that same year; and through revenue service on the newly constructed Chicago "airline" began on April 29, 1888. Still in the future were the present "Transcon" line through Amarillo and Belen, the San Joaquin Valley line to San Francisco, the "Peavine" line from Ash Fork to Phoenix, and the Coleman cutoff providing direct service from Texas to the west. But in 1889, the Official Guide still shows the constituent parts of the railroad as separate entities—the various companies would not officially be bonded together until after the 1893 bankruptcy and subsequent reorganization—and I have reproduced that information here. However, make no mistake, in spite of the separate names they were a single unit under a single top management, and the separate company names all appear together in sequential pages of the Guide.
