The Royal Palm was Southern's heavyweight workhorse on the Cincinnati-Jacksonville run (and it appears on this timetable as the connecting train for the Chicago-Jacksonville through sleeper below). However, it was to inspire this streamlined winter-season-only sibling, the New Royal Palm, which made its debut on December 17, 1949, offering two-night-out service between major cities of the Great Lakes region and Florida. The new streamliner would gather its inbound cars from Detroit, Chicago, Cleveland, and Buffalo via various overnight New York Central trains at its northern terminal in Cincinnati, and proceed southbound through Southern's scenic "Rathole" division (so named because of its many tunnels) to Chattanooga, thence via Atlanta and overnight to a connection in Jacksonville with the Florida East Coast, which operated the train along the Florida beaches south to Miami.[*]

The timetable below also reproduces in full three of the New York Central's connecting trains, the Ohio Special/Michigan Special between Detroit and Cincinnati, the Midnight Special (yes, there really was a Midnight Special) between Cleveland, Columbus, and Cincinnati, and a nameless overnight train linking Cincinnati and Chicago. This gives you a representative snapshot of New York Central's extensive overnight service in the upper Midwest, circa 1951.

The New Royal Palm would continue to operate during the winter seasons through 1955, after which time much of its equipment would be shifted to the original Royal Palm as it upgraded to streamliner status.
